Hi there! My name is Adam Paul, and I'm a junior-year Software Engineering major at Florida Gulf Coast University. Though this is my first time seeking out my Bachelor's degree, I graduated from both high school and college in May of 2019, obtaining a high school diploma and an Associate in Arts degree simultaneously. Since transferring to FGCU at the start of the Fall semester in 2019, I've been wholeheartedly devoting myself to my studies, improving myself personally and professionally in the process. Since the start of this calendar year, I've been working as a Teaching Assistant for many classes in FGCU's Mathematics department, utilizing my own knowledge of math as a tool to assist in the instruction of my students. In my free time I enjoy attending Software Engineering events, such as meetings for the Software Engineering Club at my college or marathon-programming "Hackathons." In fact, I attended my first Hackathon in October of 2020, wherein three friends and I were able to create a piece of software that could encrypt and decrypt both words and images called CipherIt!

My primary motivation for becoming an engineer ties into a very key part of my personal identity: my own disability. As someone who has been physically disabled since the age of nine, I've been working with adaptive technology for more than half of my life. This very technology, along with the option of Florida Virtual School, is what made the continuation of my education possible in the first place. However, I'll be the first to admit that much of this technology is out of date or simply too lacking in adaptive features. Seeking the ability to change this, combined with my affinity for technology, naturally drew me into the field of software engineering. Thus, my long-term goal as a software engineer is to redesign and fine-tune much of the widely-distributed, education-related, adaptive software in circulation today to be more in-line with the needs of the disabled.

Though I have a well-defined long-term goal professionally, my short-term goals are all academic. The goal of the highest priority for me is to graduate college with a 4.0 GPA. As my academics have always been extremely important to me, this goal is especially important to me personally. Aside from that, I also strive to improve further my skills in the Python and Java programming languages, continuously improving my understanding of Object-Oriented Programming along the way. Lastly, I also hope to involve myself in as many student projects as possible, making new connections with peers and professionals alike in the process.

CipherIt Encryption Generation Project


This work-in-progress Java-based encryption project allows the user four main options: encrypting a string of text the user inputs, decrypting a string of text the user inputs, encrypting an image, and decrypting an image. This project was built by a small team of four during the Knight Hacks 2020 hackatahon. Though this project is currently unfinished, our team of four plans to add several more features to make the piece of software both functional and educational. After refining the image encryption and decryption algorithms, we plan to add an option to allow the user to save encrypted images for later decryption. Additionally, we plan to refine our text log system to explain how each cipher works after a given decryption.

Snake Game Project


This project is a recreation of the classic Snake game, localized entirely in the console window. This C++ project served as the main project I worked on when I learned the language in my Programming Methodology class. The game features full collision detection on both the snake and the screen's boundaries, which are represented visually as white lines. Additionally, the food that allows the snake to grow is randomly generated within the boundaries of the game, making for a different experience evey time you play it! The project demonstrates knowledge of multiple fundamental programming concepts, such as pointers, pass by reference, and custom classes/headers.

Summary

Overall, I found this class to be unlike any other I've taken at FGCU. Through it, I was able to build critical analysis skills that I have been unable to hone in any of my software engineering courses so far. Additionally, though the group assignments could cause much grief in scenarios where you're placed with an uncooperative group, I always felt that any given group I was a part of was able to respect one another on a level I've not experienced in other classes. Overall, I feel that the conduct involved in the exchange of ideas within this course is a skill I can carry over to my work as a professional in the field of software engineering.

Future Solutions: A Smartphone for the Disabled



As a disabled person myself, I always make an effort to spot ways in which technology I use daily might be unusable for the disabled. Thus, when my team and I were discussing project ideas for a conceptual project that solves a real problem, smartphones came to mind near-instantly. While smartphoens have changed our lives significantly over the past decade, the fact remains that many people can't experience all the benefits that they provide. For example, the blind cannot navigate screens effectively and the deaf are unable to partake in regular phone calls. Thus, we decided that a smartphone designed for disabled people would be the perfect project idea. The smartphone would have advanced accessibility features such as speech-to-text, text-to-speech, eye-control, voice control, and more. Further, the phone would be support accessibility-related peripheral devices via USB, adding more functionality such as blinking lights depending on what the peripheral developer designed. Overall, I felt that this project both gave us a wider appreciation of all the technology that we're able to use daily and gave myself and my teammates a more critical eye as to what truly makes a product accessible.

Sustainability


1) The concept of the Three Pillars of Sustainability was an extremely interesting one to me. For me, the most common connection I made with the word "sustainability" was solely its environmental one. However, to simplify the matter to such a degree is disingenuous. This course reminded me that sustainable efforts are not only ones that benefit the environment, but also promote economic development and social prosperity.


2) The Play Pumps project was a stark reminder that not all well-intentioned ideas are truly sustainable. At first, I thought the idea was brilliant, combining a fundamental need for water with a fun activity for children. However, while the people behind the pumps may have had the best of intentions, their lack of planning led the a quick downfall for the project. This shocking lack of consideration has stuck with me, serving as a reminder as to how not to handle a project.


3) In the Climate Policy assignment, we got to take a look at sustainability-related efforts through the eyes of a politician. We were able to see the motivations and justifications for some of the most common public policy mechanisms that we knew of! In doing so, I felt that I gained a greater appreciation for the efforts of city projects related to sustainability. The mechanisms presented, comprising of many projects we often don't even think about, were rather intricate in their reasoning, commanding respect for the ones who presented them in the first place.
